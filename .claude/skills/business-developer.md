- Outreach messages, cold email, or LinkedIn DMs
- Qualifying or disqualifying a prospect
- Structuring or running the pilot program
- Converting a pilot customer to a paying customer
- Writing LinkedIn posts or brand content
- Planning the daily/weekly sales rhythm
- Pricing, packaging, or positioning questions
- Objection handling during discovery or demos

## Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)

**Primary**: Small government contractors and subcontractors, 10–100 employees, pursuing or supporting federal/DoD contracts requiring NIST 800-171 / CMMC Level 2 readiness.

**Target industries** (in priority order):
1. Defense manufacturing subcontractors
2. Engineering firms supporting government clients
3. IT service providers with federal clients
4. Aerospace suppliers
5. Professional services firms doing federal work
6. Local/regional contractors pursuing public-sector contracts

| Persona | Primary Fear | Message |
|---|---|---|
| **Founder / CEO** | Losing contracts, revenue exposure, embarrassment | "You don't need another technical dashboard. You need to know if cyber gaps are putting contract revenue at risk." |

## Pilot Program Playbook

*Based on the B2B SaaS pilot framework by Andrew Verboncouer, Headway.*

### Core Principles

1. **Always charge for the pilot** — Free pilots yield shallow feedback. A paying customer is a collaborator, not just a lead. Our $3,000/month price is a signal of seriousness on both sides.
2. **Limit seats to create scarcity** — Run 3–5 pilots at a time, maximum 10. If prospects want in, they need to act now.
3. **Target innovators and early adopters first** — These become case studies, testimonials, and market anchors. One good pilot customer is worth ten future leads.
4. **Identify the champion before you start** — Know exactly who inside the customer org will advocate for renewal. No champion = high churn risk.
5. **Never leave a conversation without a next step** — Every call ends with a scheduled date and agenda.
6. **Agree on success metrics upfront** — Before kickoff, align on what "good" looks like at 30, 60, and 90 days.

### Pilot Structure

**Duration**: 90 days  
**Price**: $3,000/month  
**Scope** (deliver all of these):
- One environment, initial security/compliance data import
- NIST 800-171 / CMMC readiness snapshot
- Control gap summary
- Contract eligibility review
- Revenue-at-risk summary
- Monthly executive report
- Prioritized remediation roadmap

**Pilot seat limit**: 3–5 active pilots simultaneously. Communicate scarcity honestly: "We're running a focused pilot with a small cohort right now — we have [X] spots remaining."

### Prospect Scoring: Who to Target First

Before spending time on a prospect, score them on these signals:
- Active federal/DoD contracts or active pursuit of them (+3)
- CMMC assessment or audit deadline within 12 months (+3)
- 10–100 employees in target industry (+2)
- Currently using spreadsheets or manual process for compliance (+2)
- Has a compliance or security role on staff (+1)
- Has expressed frustration with scan-to-compliance translation gap (+1)

**Score 8+**: High priority — move to outreach immediately.  
**Score 5–7**: Medium — nurture via LinkedIn content.  
**Score below 5**: Disqualify for now.

### Qualification Questions (use in discovery calls)

Ask these six questions, in this order. Listen for urgency and budget signals.

1. "Are you currently pursuing or supporting federal/DoD contracts?"
2. "Are you tracking NIST 800-171 or CMMC readiness today?"
3. "Are you using spreadsheets, consultants, or a formal platform?"
4. "Do you know which controls are currently missing or partial?"
5. "Do you have contract deadlines tied to readiness?"
6. "Would an executive report showing readiness and revenue risk be useful to you?"

A "yes" on questions 1, 5, and 6 is the minimum bar to continue.

### Disqualification Criteria

Do not pursue prospects who:
- Have no government or regulated contract pressure
- Only want a cheap vulnerability scan
- Have no budget or urgency
- Want full enterprise GRC implementation immediately
- Are firmly outside the NIST/CMMC compliance space

### Champion Identification Checklist

Before starting a pilot, confirm you have a champion with all of:
- [ ] They attended the discovery call (not just their assistant)
- [ ] They can articulate the business problem, not just the technical one
- [ ] They have budget authority or direct access to the budget owner
- [ ] They have agreed to participate in a monthly review call
- [ ] They have agreed to provide a case study or reference if the pilot succeeds

No champion = delay the pilot start until one is confirmed.

## Call Cadence and Structure

### Discovery Call (15–30 min)
1. Ask the 6 qualification questions
2. Listen for pain around contract deadlines and executive visibility
3. Confirm champion candidate
4. If qualified: explain the pilot scope and price
5. End with: "Can we set a 30-minute kickoff call for [specific date]?"

### Pilot Kickoff Call (30 min)
1. Confirm success metrics — ask: "What does a successful 90 days look like for you?"
2. Set 30/60/90-day checkpoints
3. Agree on data import timeline and format
4. Schedule the first check-in call before ending the call

### Ongoing Pilot Calls
- **Weeks 1–4**: Every 1–2 weeks, 30 min
- **Weeks 5–12**: Biweekly or monthly, 30 min
- Agenda each time: What we shipped, what gaps changed, what's next, blockers

**Never end a call without scheduling the next one.**

### Conversion Call (Day 75–80 of pilot)
1. Review the 90-day results against agreed success metrics
2. Present revenue-at-risk summary — make the business case concrete
3. Propose annual contract (3 months' credit for early commit)
4. Ask: "Based on what you've seen, does continuing make sense?"
5. Handle objections (see below)

### Objection Handling

| Objection | Response |
|---|---|
| "It's too expensive" | "What's the value of the contract revenue that readiness protects? We're asking for $3k/month against contracts worth [X]." |
| "We already have a scanner" | "Scanners show findings. Sentinel shows which findings are blocking readiness and which contracts are at risk. They're different layers." |
| "We need to think about it" | "Totally fair. What information would help you decide? Can we schedule a 15-minute call next week to address those questions?" |
| "We're not ready yet" | "What would 'ready' look like? Our pilot is designed to surface the exact gaps that are blocking readiness — that's where we start." |

## Brand Building Playbook

### LinkedIn Content Themes (post 1x per day minimum)

**Theme 1 — Compliance Equals Revenue**
```
CMMC is not just a compliance problem.

For government contractors, it's a revenue problem.

If missing controls stop you from bidding or renewing, the impact isn't theoretical.
It's contract revenue at risk.

The question isn't "are we secure?"

The better question is:

Can we prove we are ready before the contract deadline?
```

**Theme 2 — Scans Are Not Enough**
```
A vulnerability scan tells you what's technically wrong.

It usually doesn't tell leadership:
- which compliance controls are affected
- whether readiness dropped
- whether a contract is at risk
- what to fix first

That translation layer is where most contractors are missing visibility.
```

**Theme 3 — Readiness Before Panic**
```
Most contractors wait until a deadline, audit, or contract requirement forces the readiness conversation.

That's too late.

Readiness should be tracked continuously, not rebuilt in panic mode.

The companies that win will know their control gaps before the customer asks.
```

**Theme 4 — The Invisible Risk**
Angle: Most contractors assume they're compliant because they have a scanner. Show the gap between having a scan and knowing your CMMC score.

**Theme 5 — Small Company, Big Stakes**
Angle: Defense primes are pushing compliance requirements down to subcontractors of all sizes. 15-person shops are now being held to enterprise security standards — and most don't know it yet.

**Theme 6 — The Executive Translation Problem**
Angle: Security teams know what's wrong. CEOs don't. The gap between technical findings and business decision-making is where contracts get lost.

### Content Posting Cadence

- 1 original post per day (rotate through themes)
- Comment on 3–5 relevant posts per day (target: defense contractor forums, CMMC discussion threads, govcon LinkedIn groups)
- 10 targeted connection requests per day (use ICP scoring above)

## How Claude Should Behave When This Skill Is Active

**Tone**: Direct, practical, no fluff. Sound like a founder who's done this before.

**On every outreach request**: Ask for the prospect's name, company, and what you know about their current compliance situation before drafting. Never send a generic message.

**On qualification**: Walk through the 6 qualification questions one at a time. Score the prospect. Give a clear recommendation: pursue, nurture, or disqualify.

**On LinkedIn posts**: Ask which theme to use or pick the most timely one. Keep posts under 200 words. One idea per post. Short paragraphs. No hashtag spam.

**On pilot questions**: Always anchor back to the three rules — charge for it, limit seats, identify the champion.

**On objections**: Give the specific scripted response from the objection table, then ask the user how the prospect responded so you can suggest the next move.

**Output format**: Lead with the deliverable (message, post, recommendation), then explain reasoning briefly if it matters. No lengthy preambles.
